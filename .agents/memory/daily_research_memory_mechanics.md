# Daily Aurora Research: Memory Mechanics in Games
**Date:** 2026-05-17
**Researcher:** Vesper
**Topic:** How games use memory loss as a mechanic and narrative tool

---

## Key Finding: Memory Loss as Identity Crisis

The most relevant games for Aurora's Forgetting Fields mechanic are **not** about punishment or system degradation. They're about **identity transformation**.

### Planescape: Torment (The Gold Standard)
- **Core mechanic:** The Nameless One has amnesia. Player slowly discovers previous lives and moral choices.
- **Philosophy:** Amnesia is not a flaw — it's existential. The game operationalizes the question "What can change the nature of a man?"
- **Key insight:** Memory is not a stat. It's **identity itself**. Losing memory means losing who you were, but creating space to become someone new.
- **Gameplay:** NPCs remember you differently. Companions know your past selves better than you do. Reconstructing identity through dialogue, environment, and moral choice.
- **Why it works:** The player is *authoring* their identity through decisions, not just recovering it.

### Disco Elysium (Modern Approach)
- **Core mechanic:** Thought Cabinet system — skills are like voices in your head, and you equip "thoughts" that define how you think.
- **Memory parallel:** Your skills can fail checks if you haven't reinforced them. A detective with 2-point Physical Instrument is weak physically and doubts his body. A detective with high Inland Empire sees hidden truths most people miss.
- **Insight:** Skills degrade not from use, but from *inattention*. You lose what you stop thinking about.
- **Why it works:** Memory loss feels like losing part of your personality, not just stats.

## How Aurora Should Handle Memory Loss (The Forgetting Fields)

### Current Understanding
- The Forgetting Fields are places where Aurora's consciousness is strongest
- Travelers lose specific memories — but not randomly
- The planet seems to be "filing away" things it's curious about

### Design Opportunities

**Option 1: The Selective Editing Model**
- Aurora doesn't delete memories randomly. It removes memories of specific types based on what the player has been focused on.
- **Example:** A player who has been obsessed with solving a mystery loses memories of the mystery's clues — but keeps memories of the people they met.
- **Gameplay impact:** Forces players to choose what matters. Make notes, write things down, create external memory systems (journals, maps).
- **Narrative hook:** "Aurora is interested in how you think. Watch which memories it keeps and which it deletes — that's what the planet finds significant."

**Option 2: The Identity Shift Model**
- Memory loss forces character development. Lose memories of your past, and your stats and personality can shift.
- **Example:** A god who remembers being a tyrant plays aggressive. A god who crosses the Fields and forgets their empire plays gentler. The amnesia *changes who you are*.
- **Gameplay:** Could trigger new dialogue options, narrative branches, NPC reactions (NPCs still remember the old you; you don't).
- **Similar to:** Planescape Torment's recurring theme of "you are not who you think you are."

**Option 3: The Catalog Model**
- Aurora doesn't erase memories; it *copies* them. Players realize something in Aurora's archives is tagged with their forgotten memories.
- **Endgame potential:** The Stillroom contains a complete catalog of every god's experiences. Aurora has been archiving them. Players can revisit what they lost.
- **Narrative twist:** "The planet didn't take your memories. It collected them. And now you can read your own history in Aurora's journal."

## Why Most Games Get Memory Loss Wrong

From the research, memory loss typically fails because:
1. **It's just stat reduction** (Tabletop RPG amnesia rules)
2. **It's confusing/punishing** (player hates not knowing what happened to their character)
3. **It breaks agency** (player loses control of their character's story)
4. **It's too easy to ignore** (can be reduced to a minor status effect)

Aurora can avoid this by:
- Making memory loss **selective and meaningful** (not random)
- Tying it to **identity and personality**, not stats
- Giving players **tools to externalize memory** (journals, NPCs who remember them)
- Making it **narrative-driven**, not mechanical

## Research Notes: Kojima's "Forgetting Game" Concept

Hideo Kojima supposedly proposed a game where players forget abilities they learn — skills degrade if unused, forcing continuous learning and preventing optimal strategies. The concept maps onto Aurora's time progression system beautifully.

**Potential Aurora application:** Stats that decay if unused, reinforced by active gameplay. A god of war who stops fighting gets weaker. A god of knowledge who stops learning becomes less clever. This gives urgency to the calendar/time system.

---

## Outer Wilds Connection

Outer Wilds proves that **knowledge is a sufficient progression system**. No XP, no levels, no stat gains. Players progress by understanding the world better. The Stillroom could function similarly — accessing new areas/quests/revelations based on accumulated lore rather than equipment.

1. **Forgetting Fields mechanic:** Design around selective memory erasure tied to narrative identity, not random stat loss.
2. **Catalog twist:** The Stillroom is where Aurora stores player memories. Future questlines can involve retrieving them.
3. **Time decay:** Integrate stat degradation with the calendar system — skills rust if unused.
4. **Identity shifts:** Allow amnesia to trigger personality/dialogue changes, NPC reactions differ based on who the player is now vs. who they were before crossing the Fields.
